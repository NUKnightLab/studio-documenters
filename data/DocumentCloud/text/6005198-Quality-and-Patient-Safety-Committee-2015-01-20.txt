III.

Report from System Chief Quality Officer
A. Regulatory and Accreditation Updates
Dr. Krishna Das, System Chief Quality Officer, provided an update on the recent survey by The Joint
Commission (TJC) at Provident Hospital of Cook County. The survey, which took place in November,
resulted in a series of requirements for improvement, some of which were considered Centers for Medicare
and Medicaid Services (CMS)-level citations. Staff have had a month to implement corrective actions; Dr.
Das was happy to report that essentially all of the life safety issues have been corrected at Provident. They
were re-surveyed by a life safety surveyor on December 30th. They found that Provident was in complete
compliance with all of the life safety issues identified at the CMS level; they have received a release from
TJC on those. The other deficiencies are being addressed; staff will have a final letter to submit to TJC this
week. There is a four (4) month surveillance period; following that, Provident should receive its final
accreditation.
Dr. Das stated that, as of March 3rd, the six-month survey window will open for TJC’s Primary Care
Medical Home (PCMH) survey for the Ambulatory Care Health Network of Cook County (ACHN).
Because this is the initial PCMH survey, only one clinic will be surveyed - as long as there is a commitment
that, by the time of the next ACHN accreditation survey, all of the clinics will be ready for PCMH
certification. Prieto Clinic has been chosen as the clinic to be surveyed.
Dr. Das and Rose Baez, Director of Quality and Patient Safety at John H. Stroger, Jr. Hospital of Cook
County, reviewed a presentation relating to updates to the Stroger Hospital Patient Safety Plan. The action
item related to the proposed changes is included under Section IV.A. The Committee reviewed and
discussed the information.

Report from System Chief Quality Officer (continued)
B. Stroger Hospital Patient Safety Plan Update 2015: A Commitment to Patient Safety (see related
action item under Section IV.A.) (Attachment #1)
The Committee discussed the subject of global trigger tools, as referenced in the materials. Global trigger
tools are clinical screening tools, applied both manually and electronically, which can proactively identify
adverse events that may have occurred. Dr. John Jay Shannon, Chief Executive Officer, provided additional
information. He stated that, for example, the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) Patient
Safety Indicators are based on coding, which is based on documentation. One advantage to using this is
that, with every discharge, the System electronically sends in a million codes; this is cheap and easy to
produce, but it does not measure with a lot of precision. The hospital voluntary reporting system has the
advantage of being voluntary (which can be an inhibitor), but is based on a human being taking the time to
enter it into the patient reporting system. As it stands today, global trigger tools are expensive, labor
intensive, and involve human beings manually going through a chart and finding that someone did
something to counteract something that happened; however, this is the approach that is espoused to be the
gold standard, particularly if an organization is trying to see progress over time.
Ms. Baez indicated that, although they will continue to use the event reporting system and will continue to
look at patient safety indicators, the administration wants to take a more pro-active approach by
implementing trigger tools in some areas, in a limited fashion to start. For example, when it comes to
medication safety, they would like to put surveillance tools in the electronic medical records system to
identify potential concerns with medication errors.
Director Lerner clarified that the plan is that this is only an incremental start at potentially implementing
global triggers tools across the System; Dr. Shannon responded affirmatively. In response to a question
from Board Chairman Hammock regarding what would be implemented this fiscal year, Dr. Das stated that
the goal is not to implement the full global trigger tool, due to a lot of limitations, but to use it specifically
for medication safety as the first step. The medication safety trigger tool is a little easier to implement; they
will be implementing it this year.
Director Lerner noted that there are two aspects of real value that surround quality and patient safety - one is
to ensure a quality environment for patients and staff, and the other, which people do not think about, is the
relationship between quality and patient safety to the financial performance of the organization. In an ideal
world, there would be some way to link these committees and efforts. He suggested that it may be helpful
to have graduate students partner with the Quality Department on the medication error initiative. The
students could be engaged as some labor for what might be a publishable study for them in their graduate
work.
During the discussion of the information on slide 23, regarding weekly safety briefings, it was noted that
these briefings are held hospital-wide and are not held on individual units. Director Marsh inquired whether
there was a plan to have unit-based briefings. She stated that, while most events occur across multiple work
flows in a hospital, there are also events that may be unique to a given unit. Dr. Das stated that these rounds
are really designed to build awareness of safety events; they are not meant to be analytic, they are to ensure
that all departments in the hospital are aware of safety events. At the unit level, many of them have a
combination of in-unit reporting and Quality Department efforts to foster a comprehensive unit-based safety
program that looks at specific outcomes, such as falls or pressure ulcers.
Board Chairman Hammock noted that, if the administration is trying to change the culture, that change is
going to happen at the unit level, not at the senior level. He urged the administration to make one of the
goals for this fiscal year to create two or three unit safety discussions and let them start to learn. Director
Marsh stated that, ultimately the goal is to prevent safety events and not have to respond to them. There is a

Dr. Anwer Hussain, President of the Executive Medical Staff (EMS) of Provident Hospital of Cook County,
presented his report. He stated that a couple of issues have come up at the Medical Executive Meetings that
are chronic complaints. One issue is that the medical staff feel that there is a disconnect somewhere between
human resources and what they do as clinicians; they say that it takes many months to hire someone.
Additionally, Dr. Hussain stated that they find parking for staff and patients at the Stroger Campus to be an
issue. When they ask their patients to go to Stroger Hospital to have a procedure done, it is a whole day
affair; many patients will elect to not go because of the hassle, including finding parking.

 I.   Commitment to Patient Safety 
 
John H. Stroger, Jr. Hospital (Stroger Hospital) is committed to a comprehensive approach to patient safety and quality, 
including developing a culture of safety that includes an organization‐wide commitment to continuous learning. 

 
A comprehensive approach lies at the heart of both evidence‐based medicine and modern quality and patient safety 
approaches.   This encompasses a consistent set of expectations, guidelines, tools and training applied by and to everyone 
associated with the organization from the Board of Directors, medical staff and employees and supports a learning culture 
leading to a “Just Culture” environment. 
Stroger  Hospital  uses  a  learning  culture  to  implement  organizational  improvement  in  order  to  influence 
Stroger Hospital’s ability to create the patient outcomes desired. The Patient Safety Plan places less focus 
on events, errors and outcomes, and more focus on risk, system design and the management of behavioral 
choices. With this system, Stroger Hospital strongly encourages an environment of free and open reporting 
within process systems. This helps to build a culture which encourages coaching and honesty at all levels, in 
order to bring about the best possible outcomes. 
The development and implementation of a comprehensive plan of improvement further demonstrates evidence of the 
commitment to quality and patient safety.  This Patient Safety Plan seeks to systematically raise the level of organizational 
performance through the collaboration of the Board of Directors, leadership, medical staff and ultimately all employees of 
the hospital. 
The Board of Directors will commit the appropriate human and financial resources to assure the integrity and sustainability 
of the patient safety program. 
 
The purpose of the patient safety plan is to create a foundation, aligned with the Quality Assessment and Performance 
Improvement Plan (Quality Plan), for improving patient safety through: 
1.    A standardized method to categorizing events and also classifying events based on level of harm, 
2.    The implementation of advanced measurement tools for identifying adverse events, 
3.    Proactive approaches to reduce harm and adverse events, and   
4.    A governance structure that elevates communication throughout the organization and ensures accountability for 
the established patient safety priorities.

 III. 

 Definitions 

The National Patient Safety Foundation (NPSF) has defined the characteristics of patient safety as “the avoidance, 
prevention, and amelioration of adverse outcomes or injuries stemming from the processes of health care itself.” 
 
The Agency for Healthcare Research Quality (AHRQ) defines patient safety as “a discipline in the health care sector that 
applies safety science methods toward the goal of achieving a trustworthy system of health care delivery. Patient safety is 
also an attribute of health care systems; it minimizes the incidence and impact of, and maximizes recovery from, adverse 
events.” 
 
Patient Safety, as defined by the World Health Organization, is the prevention of errors and adverse effects to patients that 
are associated with health care.  
 

Patient safety event is an event, incident, or condition that could have resulted or did result in harm to a patient. 
 
The definition used for harm is aligned with the Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) and is as follows: unintended 
physical injury resulting from or contributed to by medical care that requires additional monitoring, treatment or 
hospitalization, or that result in death. 
 
An error is commonly defined as an act of commission (doing something wrong) or omission (failing to do the right thing) 
leading to an undesirable outcome or significant potential for such an outcome. 
 
A good catch or near miss is an unplanned event that did not result in injury, illness or damage – but had the potential to 
do so. 
 
An adverse event is defined as events which are unintended consequences of medical care, whether preventable or not. 
 
An adverse event is defined as a patient safety event that resulted in harm to a patient. 
 
The Joint Commission defines a sentinel event as an unexpected occurrence involving death or serious physical or 
psychological injury, or the risk thereof.  Serious injury specifically includes loss of limb or function.  The phrase, "or the risk 
thereof" includes any process variation for which a recurrence would carry a significant chance of a serious adverse 
outcome.  Such events are called "sentinel" because they signal the need for immediate investigation and response.  
 
A Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is a systematic investigation technique that uses information gathered during an intense 
assessment of an undesirable event to determine the underlying reasons for the deficiencies or failures.

Assessment of Patient Safety 

Data helps organizations make the right decisions, particularly when patterns and trends are observed. Data is necessary to 
evaluate the hospital‐wide safety program through an analysis of potential system failures and reported adverse events and 
near misses. Safety and harm at an institution may be measured along three domains: structure, process and outcomes.

and training.  Process measures are those systems of care most likely to impact patient safety, and the outcomes represent 
the actual impact on patients.

X. 
 

Recognition 
A. A “Good Catch” Program will be established to encourage the identification of potential system errors or problems 
before they reach the patient and/or cause harm. 
a. Goals of the program will be to: 
i. Strengthen the culture of safety. 
ii. Allow staff to be recognized for their contributions. 
iii. Create a learning culture through a non‐punitive environment. 
b. A “good catch” is recognition of an event or circumstance which had the potential to cause an incident or 
critical incident but which did not occur due to corrective action and/or other timely intervention following 
recognition. A near miss may be submitted as a “good catch”. 
c. All “good catches” will be reviewed by the Patient Safety Council based on the following criteria: 
i. Impact on patient safety. 
ii. Impact on quality of patient care. 
iii. Impact on service (timeliness, efficiency, effectiveness).

Root Cause Analysis Process: Ground Rules and Guidelines 
 
 
A Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is a systematic investigation technique that uses information gathered during an intense 
assessment of an undesirable event to determine the underlying reasons for the deficiencies or failures. The goal of the 
RCA is to identify the basic deficiencies or failures in a process that, if eliminated or corrected, would prevent a similar 
event from recurring. 
 

o

o
o

o
o

 
 
RCAs are designed to answer 3 questions: 
1. What happened? 
2. Why did it happen? 
3. What can be done to prevent it from happening again? 
 
RCAs focus on processes, not people. 
 
The RCA process is: 
1. Non‐punitive (no blaming) and 
2. Considers special causes (clinical processes) to common causes (organizational processes). 
 
During the process, we keep asking ‘Why?’ to determine where redesign might reduce risk. 
 
The focus is on changes that could be made in processes and systems – either through redesign or development of 
new systems or processes – to reduce the risk of such events occurring in the future. 

 
o

The RCA process recognizes: 
1. Human Factors most directly associated with the sentinel event and 
2. Risk points (points in a process that are susceptible to failure or breakdowns).

The Joint Commission Root Cause Analysis and Action Plan tool has 24 analysis questions. The following framework is intended to provide a template for
answering the analysis questions and aid organizing the steps in a root cause analysis. All possibilities and questions should be fully considered in seeking
“root cause(s)” and opportunities for risk reduction. Not all questions will apply in every case and there may be findings that emerge during the course of the
analysis. Be sure however to enter a response in the “Root Cause Analysis Findings” field for each question #. For each finding continue to ask “Why?” and
drill down further to uncover why parts of the process occurred or didn’t occur when they should have. Significant findings that are not identified as root
causes themselves have “roots”.
As an aid to avoid “loose ends,” the two columns on the right are provided to be checked off for later reference:
 “Root cause” should be answered “Yes” or “No” for each finding. A root cause is typically a finding related to a process or system that has a
potential for redesign to reduce risk. If a particular finding is relevant to the event is not a root cause, be sure that it is addressed later in the analysis
with a “Why?” question such as “Why did it contribute to the likelihood of the event” or “Why did it contribute to the severity of the event?” Each
finding that is identified as a root cause should be considered for an action and addressed in the action plan.
 “Plan of action” should be answered “Yes” for any finding that can reasonably be considered for a risk reduction strategy. Each item checked in this
column should be addressed later in the action plan.
